Vacuum Tubes · 1940–1956
Technology
Vacuum Tube Era
1940 — 1956
The dawn of computing. Massive machines filled entire rooms, consuming enormous amounts of electricity and generating intense heat. Vacuum tubes acted as electronic switches and amplifiers, making digital computation physically possible for the first time.

AT
Alan Turing
Father of Computer Science
Conceived the theoretical "Universal Machine" in 1936, laying the mathematical foundation for all digital computing. Cracked Nazi Enigma codes in WWII.
JN
John von Neumann
Architect of Modern Computers
Proposed the stored-program computer architecture (1945) — the Von Neumann Architecture that all modern CPUs still follow today.
JM
Mauchly & Eckert
Builders of ENIAC
Built ENIAC (1945), the first general-purpose electronic digital computer, containing 17,468 vacuum tubes and weighing 30 tons.
Transistors · 1956–1963
Technology
Transistor Revolution
1956 — 1963
The transistor — a tiny semiconductor device — replaced bulky vacuum tubes. Computers became smaller, faster, cheaper, and more reliable. Assembly language replaced machine code, and the first high-level programming languages like FORTRAN and COBOL emerged.

WS
Shockley, Bardeen & Brattain
Inventors of the Transistor
Invented the transistor at Bell Labs in 1947 (Nobel Prize 1956). This single invention triggered the entire modern electronics industry.
GH
Grace Hopper
Pioneer of Programming
Developed the first compiler (A-0, 1952) and co-created COBOL, making computers programmable by humans in readable language. Also coined the term "debugging."
JB
John Backus
Creator of FORTRAN
Led the IBM team that created FORTRAN (1957), the first widely-used high-level programming language, making scientific computing accessible.
